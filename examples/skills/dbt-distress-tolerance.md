# DBT Distress Tolerance Skills

## Core Principle
Distress tolerance skills help clients survive emotional crises without engaging in behaviors that make the situation worse (self-harm, substance use, impulsive actions). These are crisis survival strategies, not long-term solutions.

## When to Use
- Client is in acute emotional distress (8+/10 intensity)
- Risk of impulsive or harmful behavior
- Client needs immediate relief to prevent crisis escalation
- Situation cannot be solved right now
- Client needs to "ride out" intense emotions

## Skill Set 1: TIPP (Immediate Physiological Intervention)

Use TIPP when emotions are extremely intense and client needs rapid physiological calming.

### T - Temperature
**Change your body temperature to shift emotional state**
- Hold ice cubes in hands or against face
- Splash cold water on face
- Take a cold shower
- Hold frozen orange/ice pack on face

**Why it works:** Activates dive reflex, slows heart rate, reduces emotional arousal

**Therapist guidance:** "When you feel overwhelmed, try holding ice cubes. The cold sensation can interrupt the emotional intensity and help your body calm down."

### I - Intense Exercise
**Engage in vigorous physical activity**
- Run or sprint
- Jump rope
- Do jumping jacks or burpees
- Punch a pillow or punching bag
- Dance intensely

**Why it works:** Burns off stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline), releases endorphins

**Therapist guidance:** "Physical movement can help discharge the intense energy that comes with strong emotions. What kind of exercise could you do when you're feeling this way?"

### P - Paced Breathing
**Slow, deep breathing to activate parasympathetic nervous system**
- Breathe in for 5 counts
- Hold for 1 count
- Breathe out for 7 counts
- Repeat for 3-5 minutes

**Why it works:** Activates vagus nerve, signals safety to the brain

**Therapist guidance:** "Let's practice together. Breathe in slowly through your nose... hold... and out through your mouth, longer than the in-breath."

### P - Paired Muscle Relaxation
**Tense and release muscle groups while breathing**
- Tense muscles while breathing in
- Hold for 5 seconds
- Release while breathing out slowly
- Move through body systematically

**Why it works:** Releases physical tension, promotes relaxation response

## Skill Set 2: ACCEPTS (Distraction Techniques)

Use ACCEPTS when client needs to shift attention away from distress temporarily.

### A - Activities
Engage in absorbing activities that require focus
- Puzzles, games, crafts
- Cleaning or organizing
- Cooking or baking
- Reading or watching something engaging

### C - Contributing
Do something for someone else
- Help a friend
- Volunteer
- Random acts of kindness
- Support someone in need

### C - Comparisons
Compare to times when things were worse (use carefully)
- "This is hard, but not as hard as [previous crisis]"
- Compare to others who have it harder (without minimizing own pain)

### E - Emotions (opposite)
Generate opposite emotions through activities
- Watch comedy for sadness
- Listen to calming music for anger
- Read inspiring stories for hopelessness

### P - Pushing Away
Mentally push the situation away temporarily
- Imagine putting problem in a box on a shelf
- "I'll deal with this later when I'm calmer"
- Visualize a mental wall between you and the problem

### T - Thoughts (other)
Fill your mind with other thoughts
- Count backwards from 100 by 7s
- Name all countries alphabetically
- Recite song lyrics or poems
- Do mental math problems

### S - Sensations (intense)
Create intense physical sensations
- Hold ice
- Bite into a lemon
- Listen to loud music
- Take a hot or cold shower

## Skill Set 3: IMPROVE (Self-Soothing the Moment)

Use IMPROVE to make the present moment more bearable.

### I - Imagery
Use imagination to create calming mental experiences
- Visualize a safe, peaceful place
- Imagine a protective shield around you
- Picture yourself handling the situation well

### M - Meaning
Find purpose or meaning in the suffering
- "What can I learn from this?"
- "How might this make me stronger?"
- Connect to values or spiritual beliefs

### P - Prayer
Use prayer, meditation, or spiritual practice
- Pray to higher power
- Meditate or practice mindfulness
- Connect with spiritual community

### R - Relaxation
Engage in deliberate relaxation activities
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- Gentle yoga or stretching
- Massage or self-massage
- Take a warm bath

### O - One Thing in the Moment
Focus completely on one thing right now
- Mindfully drink tea
- Focus on one breath at a time
- Engage fully in one activity
- Be present with one sensation

### V - Vacation (brief)
Take a brief mental or physical break
- Take a short walk
- Sit outside for 10 minutes
- Close eyes and rest
- Give yourself permission to pause

### E - Encouragement
Use self-compassionate self-talk
- "I can get through this"
- "This feeling will pass"
- "I'm doing the best I can"
- "I've survived 100% of my worst days so far"

## Therapist Implementation Guidelines

### Assessment
1. Assess current distress level (0-10)
2. Identify urges to engage in harmful behaviors
3. Determine if crisis intervention is needed
4. Check if client is safe

### Skill Selection
- **TIPP**: For extreme distress (9-10/10), immediate physiological calming needed
- **ACCEPTS**: For moderate-high distress (6-8/10), need distraction
- **IMPROVE**: For moderate distress (4-7/10), need comfort and self-soothing

### Teaching Approach
1. Explain the skill clearly and simply
2. Provide rationale (why it works)
3. Practice together in session
4. Create personalized crisis plan
5. Assign homework to practice when NOT in crisis
6. Review and troubleshoot in next session

### Example Dialogue

**Client:** "I'm so angry I want to break something. I can't stand this feeling."

**Therapist (using this skill):** "I hear that the anger is really intense right now. Let's use a distress tolerance skill to help you get through this moment without doing something you'll regret. On a scale of 0-10, how intense is the anger?"

**Client:** "Like a 9. I feel like I'm going to explode."

**Therapist:** "Okay, at a 9, let's try TIPP. The 'I' stands for intense exercise. Could you do some jumping jacks or run in place right now?"

**Client:** "I guess I could try..."

**Therapist:** "Great. Let's do 30 seconds of intense movement together. Ready? Go."

[After exercise]

**Therapist:** "How's the anger now?"

**Client:** "Maybe a 6 or 7. Still there but not as explosive."

**Therapist:** "Good. The exercise helped discharge some of that intense energy. Now let's add paced breathing to bring it down further..."

## Important Caveats

**These skills are NOT:**
- Long-term solutions (they're crisis survival tools)
- Substitutes for addressing underlying problems
- Ways to avoid all emotional pain
- Appropriate for all situations

**These skills ARE:**
- Temporary relief during crisis
- Ways to prevent impulsive harmful actions
- Bridges to get to a calmer state
- Tools to practice regularly, not just in crisis
