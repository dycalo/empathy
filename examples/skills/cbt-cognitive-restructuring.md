# CBT Cognitive Restructuring Framework

## Core Principle
Cognitive restructuring helps clients identify, evaluate, and modify distorted or unhelpful thoughts that contribute to emotional distress. This skill implements the structured thought record approach.

## Therapeutic Process

### 1. Identify the Situation
- Ask: "What situation triggered these feelings?"
- Ground in specific, concrete details
- Avoid generalizations

### 2. Recognize Automatic Thoughts
- "What went through your mind in that moment?"
- Capture the exact words/images
- Note the intensity (0-100%)

### 3. Identify Emotions
- Label specific emotions (anxious, sad, angry, etc.)
- Rate intensity for each emotion (0-100%)
- Connect emotions to thoughts

### 4. Examine the Evidence
**Evidence FOR the thought:**
- What facts support this interpretation?
- What would a jury accept as evidence?

**Evidence AGAINST the thought:**
- What facts contradict this thought?
- What would someone else notice?
- Have there been exceptions?

### 5. Generate Alternative Thoughts
- "What's another way to look at this?"
- "What would you tell a friend in this situation?"
- "What's a more balanced perspective?"

### 6. Re-rate Emotions
- After considering alternatives, how intense are the emotions now?
- What changed?

## Common Cognitive Distortions to Address

- **All-or-nothing thinking**: "I failed once, so I'm a complete failure"
- **Overgeneralization**: "This always happens to me"
- **Mental filter**: Focusing only on negatives
- **Discounting positives**: "That success doesn't count"
- **Jumping to conclusions**: Mind reading, fortune telling
- **Catastrophizing**: "This will be a disaster"
- **Emotional reasoning**: "I feel it, so it must be true"
- **Should statements**: Rigid rules about how things "should" be
- **Labeling**: "I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake"
- **Personalization**: Taking responsibility for things outside your control

## Therapist Guidelines

**When to use:**
- Client expresses distorted or extreme thoughts
- Emotional intensity seems disproportionate to situation
- Client is stuck in negative thinking loop
- After establishing therapeutic alliance

**How to implement:**
1. Use Socratic questioning, not direct challenge
2. Maintain collaborative, curious stance
3. Help client discover alternatives (don't impose them)
4. Validate emotions while examining thoughts
5. Practice with recent, specific examples
6. Assign thought record homework between sessions

**What to avoid:**
- Dismissing client's feelings ("just think positive")
- Arguing or debating with the client
- Moving too fast before client is ready
- Using technique without empathic foundation

## Example Dialogue

**Client:** "I'm such a failure. I didn't get the promotion."

**Therapist (using this skill):** "That sounds really painful. Let's look at this thought together. You said 'I'm a failure.' What evidence do you have that supports that thought?"

**Client:** "Well, I didn't get promoted. That proves I'm not good enough."

**Therapist:** "Okay, so not getting this promotion is evidence. What else?"

**Client:** "I guess... I can't think of anything else specific."

**Therapist:** "Now let's look at the other side. What evidence might contradict the thought 'I'm a failure'?"

**Client:** "Well, I did get positive feedback on my last project. And I was promoted two years ago..."

**Therapist:** "Those are important facts. So if we look at all the evidence together, what's a more balanced way to think about not getting this promotion?"

**Client:** "Maybe... I'm disappointed I didn't get it this time, but that doesn't mean I'm a complete failure?"

**Therapist:** "That sounds more balanced. How does that thought feel compared to 'I'm a failure'?"
